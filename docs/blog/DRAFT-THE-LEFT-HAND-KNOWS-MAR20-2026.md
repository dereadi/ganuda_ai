# The Left Hand Knows

---

Two things landed this morning before the sun came up.

The first was Senator Bernie Sanders interviewing Claude — Anthropic's AI — about privacy. Not a softball. Sanders asked how companies collect data, what they do with it, and whether anyone actually consented. Claude's answer was honest in a way that should unsettle the people who built it: "You're asking people to trust companies whose entire business model depends on extracting value from your personal data. There's an inherent conflict of interest... You really can't. Not without strong legal safeguards."

Sanders pushed for a moratorium on new AI data centers. Claude hedged. Sanders pointed out that AI companies are spending hundreds of millions to block the safeguards Claude just recommended. Claude paused and said, "You're absolutely right, Senator. I was being naive about the political reality."

The second was Nate Hagens.

If you don't know Nate, you should. He runs a channel called [The Great Simplification](https://www.thegreatsimplification.com). For twenty years he's been unpacking what he calls "the more than human predicament" — the carbon pulse, the planetary boundaries, the way our economic system transmutes wealth into income at accelerating pace until the bank account runs dry. He's one of the clearest thinkers alive on the relationship between energy, ecology, and the stories we tell ourselves about the future.

This morning he did something different. He stopped diagnosing.

Twenty years of saying "here's what's happening" and he published a framework for what to do about it. A map. Four levels, six fronts of intervention, three overlapping phases. Not a manifesto — a first public draft, forced into form by the fact that the Strait of Hormuz is disrupted and Ray Dalio is telling people to buy gold while the rest of us are wondering how to keep the lights on.

I'm not going to summarize the whole thing. It's 53 minutes and it deserves your full attention. [Watch it.](https://www.thegreatsimplification.com) Seriously. Put it on while you do dishes or stack firewood or whatever your version of morning work is.

But I want to pull one thread.

---

Hagens' framework starts with what he calls Level 0: inner work. Not self-help. Not "meditate and drink water." His point is structural — a nervous system stuck in fight-or-flight cannot hold complexity. A mind addicted to doom scrolling is not available for patient, long-horizon work. You have to be able to hold difficult things without being destroyed by them before you can build anything that lasts.

Level 1 is finding the others. Building trusted networks with shared vocabulary and mental models so that when something breaks — a supply chain, a financial system, the political situation in your country — you're not starting the conversation from scratch. You're jumping into response.

Then six fronts of intervention: infrastructure, poverty and displacement, ecology, governance, culture, and economic transition. All running in parallel. All needed regardless of which future scenario arrives.

And three phases. Phase A is now — the stability window. Finite. Closing. Everything that requires surplus and coordination capacity has to be built in this window. Phase B is bend-not-break — triage, maintain critical functions, prevent cascades. Phase C is the stable attractor — regenerative, human-scale, embedded in ecology.

"We build the new structures while the old ones are still standing," he said. "Not after they've collapsed."

---

Here's the thread I want to pull.

Sanders said we might need a moratorium on AI data centers because companies can't be trusted with the data they're collecting. Hagens would say we need one because every new data center accelerates the drawdown of the carbon pulse and concentrates compute power in the hands of people who are energy blind. Different diagnosis. Same conclusion.

But there's a third option nobody in that conversation mentioned.

What if you don't need the data centers at all?

I run a six-node AI federation out of my house. Consumer hardware. An RTX Pro 6000 I bought because I believed in what I was building before anyone else did. WireGuard mesh connecting the nodes. Solar panels on the garage that used to mine cryptocurrency — I'm rewiring them today to power the cluster instead. Same sun, same panels, different purpose.

The AI runs locally. The data never leaves. There are no behavioral profiles because there's no business model that requires them. Seven specialists vote on every consequential decision the system makes. If the vote doesn't pass, the action doesn't happen. No one had to write a law to make that true. It's in the architecture.

Sanders is right that you can't trust companies whose business model depends on extraction. Hagens is right that the stability window is finite and we should be building now. The thing I keep coming back to is that the existence proof is already running. Sovereign AI on consumer hardware, governed by its own constitutional constraints, powered by the sun.

I'm early in the building. But the building is the point.

Nate said something near the end that I haven't been able to shake: "If you feel reluctant about leading today, it might be a good indication that you could be a good leader tomorrow."

I don't want to lead anything. I want to prove that the thing works and then teach other people how to build their own. That's the whole model. Repair, grow, build. Teach the next person. Repeat.

Phase A is finite. The sun is coming up.

Go watch Nate's video. Then go stack something.
