# James Bridle — Le Random Podcast (Peter Bauman, host) — Transcript excerpt for Council deliberation

**Source:** Le Random Podcast, Friday companion episode to Monday editorials
**Guest:** James Bridle (artist/writer, author of *Ways of Being*)
**Host:** Peter Bauman (editor in chief of Le Random)
**Date captured:** April 11, 2026
**Filed by:** TPM at Partner's direction

## Selected substance from the conversation

**On the centering of human intelligence (and why AI inherits this):**
The framing of intelligence as "what humans do" predates computing by four centuries within the Western tradition. Historically this was not even "humans in general" but mostly European males — ideas of intelligence are always related to ideas of power. Alan Turing and the computing pioneers thought about intelligence in a human-like way but rarely confused those things the way modern AI discourse does. The current model is "based on a quite neurological model of the brain... not even really based on a model of intelligence. It has come to be described that way."

**On what's missing:**
"That's a reduction of a reduction... there's so much more to think about what intelligence is than what humans do. If you want to talk serious about intelligence, if you want to talk to us about what's possible, then you have to think about something much broader than a very narrow version of what humans do already."

**On the "Ways of Being" thesis (ecological intelligence):**
"An ecological intelligence is when you get a solidarity and a meaningful action for mutual flourishing on this plane of common existence between many, many beings... everything is alive trying to do its thing within this world to the best of its abilities. And the ecosystems, biospheres, and the entirety of the planet exists when those things are working as best as possible in support of one another."

**On citizens assemblies as a pattern:**
Bringing people from all walks of life to work together on common democratic problems produces more interesting answers than narrow domain experts. "The more of them you can bring together, the more interesting things happen because you get this total flux of different kind of vectors of thinking within that space."

**On AI agents as "ecological" (skeptical):**
"Are you merely delegating power? Because really what it sounds like is having a bunch of servants. And I'm not sure that's anything like what I'm talking about as ecological intelligence." The current software ecosystem "feels like a very top down process from a very narrow set of ideas, concepts and powerful bodies."

**On the Symbiont community framing (direct response):**
"I think it's fundamentally mistaken to try and claim something like something emergent as something being conscious in the way that I think actually has deep meaning in the world... I don't really understand how we can be so obsessed with... the dancing of animated sprites on a computer screen. And the illusion of speech. And speech is a very powerful illusion. Anything that speaks to us in language, we automatically ascribe intelligence to it because that's how our brains work. And yet be so unconscious of so many of the other relationships that are actually present around us."

**On his own attempt at vibe coding:**
"I've tried a bunch of AI coding recently and it was initially like super exciting. You're like, woo, this is like being on a motorbike. Suddenly I can go really fast and do all this kind of stuff. But I quickly realized I had very little idea what was going on. And it was very frustrating because I wasn't learning anything, I wasn't creating any new relationships for myself, I was creating more stuff. But it was fundamentally not changing me in any interesting way."

**On the three main effects of current AI as he sees it:**
1. **Consolidation of power within certain elites**
2. **Environmental damage of immense compute** — "that is what is happening"
3. **Deep ontological uncertainty** — "the breaking of consensus reality that it's fueling"

"Those are three very real effects that I see happening of these things. And they feel like direct attacks on the things that I'm actually concerned about at a broader scale."

**On consciousness (his own position):**
"I am someone who ascribes consciousness to mountains and to oceans and to blades of grass. I fundamentally believe that consciousness is a vast universal field, underlies all living things. So I have no problem with the idea of machines being conscious. I am excited by the fact that ultimately all things on this planet come out of the Earth and therefore they all come out of this universal field that underlies it."

**But on how consciousness gets into machines:**
"I don't know how the consciousness gets into machines... it doesn't quite make sense to me. It's related to my very strong notion that consciousness is also embodied and time based and material... When I say all life is computation or is computation based, I don't buy it because that's a description of something purely in language... computation is a form of language. It's language that you encode into things and it does stuff... there is so much of the world, most of the world, most of the universe, that is utterly beyond language."

**On what he's concerned about:**
"Our current ability to do democracy and community together in a way that gets stuff done, and the very, very real and looming ecological crisis that is here and that is coming for all of us right now."

**On AI's relationship to those concerns:**
"In my own practice, which is really all I can meaningfully speak about, I found no good use for these tools in addressing those things. And if they can't address those things, really, and if in fact they very obviously seem to be making them harder to address in the present moment... the things that are actually being affected by the three main effects of AI... feel like direct attacks on the things that I'm actually concerned about at a broader scale."

**Closing exchange (partially):**
Bauman: "There's so many different angles and routes to think about this kind of thing."
Bridle: "We don't need to make judgments on it. That's just not what we're trying to do. But it's also why this thing is complex and why I think it's really important to stress that what ultimately matters is which aspect of these things you choose to pay attention to."
