## Notes Strategy

### Purpose

Substack Notes are short-form posts that appear in followers' feeds and can be discovered by non-followers through restacks, likes, and algorithmic surfacing. They are the amplification layer — hooks that pull readers into the long-form posts.

Notes are not summaries. They are not advertisements. They are standalone provocations that happen to have a longer piece behind them.

### Pattern: 3 Notes Per Post

After each post publishes, create 3 Notes within 48 hours:

1. **The Question** — A provocative question from the post, no answer. Let it sit. The reader's own curiosity should do all the lifting. If you feel the urge to explain, that is the urge to resist.

2. **The Quote** — The most striking 1-2 sentences from the post. No commentary needed. If the sentence doesn't hit on its own, pick a different sentence.

3. **The Builder Hook** — "Here's what I actually built" teaser that connects theory to practice. This is where the Tulip test lives — let them discover they're inside the architecture. Show a corner of the thing without explaining the thing.

### Timing

- **Note 1 (Question)**: Same day as publish, 2-4 hours after. The post is fresh. The question extends the conversation before the reader has moved on.
- **Note 2 (Quote)**: Next day, morning. People scroll Notes with coffee. A clean sentence lands differently at 7 AM than at 3 PM.
- **Note 3 (Builder)**: Next day, afternoon/evening. By now the question and quote have primed. The builder hook catches the people who think, not just read.

### Voice Rules

- No hashtags. No "check out my new post!" energy. No call-to-action language.
- Questions, not statements. Let the reader's curiosity do the work.
- If it sounds like marketing, delete it. Read it out loud. If you'd scroll past it, so will they.
- One idea per Note. No threads. No "1/3" numbering. Each Note stands alone.
- Jimmy the Tulip: calm, observational, slightly amused. Say less than you know. Walk away before you over-explain.
- No emojis. No exclamation marks. Periods and question marks only.

## Cross-Posting Strategy

### ganuda.us to Substack

- Every ganuda.us blog post gets a Substack version. Not a copy-paste — an adaptation.
- Substack version can be shorter, more personal, more question-oriented. ganuda.us is the technical record. Substack is the conversation.
- Substack readers want to feel addressed. ganuda.us readers want to understand the system. Same material, different posture.
- The Substack version should feel like sitting across a table from someone. The ganuda.us version should feel like reading their notebook.

### Substack to ganuda.us

- Link back to ganuda.us for technical details, architecture diagrams, code references.
- "Full write-up at ganuda.us" serves readers who want depth without cluttering the Substack piece.
- Do not force the link. If the Substack piece stands alone, let it stand alone. The link is for the curious, not a sales funnel.

### What Never Crosses

- Internal node names, IPs, infrastructure details never appear on Substack. Deer and Crawdad own this gate.
- Sacred thermals and internal governance records stay inside. The public sees the philosophy and the outcomes, not the wiring.
- If the Tulip test fails — if you're explaining the architecture instead of letting them discover it — pull back.

## Metrics to Watch

| Metric | Why It Matters |
|--------|---------------|
| Note engagement (likes, comments, restacks) | Shows which hook type resonates. Restacks are the real signal — someone put their reputation behind it. |
| Post views from Notes vs. direct/email | Measures whether Notes are actually pulling readers in or just performing to existing audience. |
| Subscriber growth rate per post | The number that actually compounds. One post that converts is worth ten that get liked. |
| Which Note type drives most engagement | After 5-6 posts, pattern should emerge. Question vs. Quote vs. Builder — double down on what works, but keep all three for variety. |
| Comment quality | Engagement count is vanity. Are the comments from builders, researchers, thinkers? Or drive-by reactions? Quality of conversation matters more than volume. |

### Review Cadence

- After every post: note which Note got the most traction. 30-second observation, not a report.
- After 5 posts: first real pattern review. Enough data to see if one hook type consistently outperforms.
- Monthly: subscriber growth rate, referral sources, comment quality. Deer reports to council, one paragraph.

## Anti-Patterns (What Not to Do)

- **No engagement bait.** "What do you think?" at the end of every Note is the Substack equivalent of a laugh track.
- **No self-congratulation.** "Just published my latest piece" is invisible. Nobody cares that you wrote something. They care if it makes them think.
- **No thread games.** "Here are 7 things I learned about X" threads belong on the platform formerly known as Twitter. One idea. One Note.
- **No frequency anxiety.** Three Notes per post. That is the cadence. Do not post Notes just to stay visible. Silence between posts is fine. The writing should pull them back, not the notification count.
- **No explaining the joke.** If the Note needs a parenthetical to land, it is the wrong Note.
